# COPY-RULES.md

Read this before touching any user-facing copy in the System Check.

These rules were derived the hard way, over a full rewrite of all 37 questions and the results copy. They are not style preferences. Each one exists because something in the tool was quietly contradicting the book it serves.

Ioana owns voice and final wording. Agents draft; she tunes. If a rule and her ear disagree, her ear wins - she is the non-native reader this tool is partly written for, and she has been right every time the two have collided.

## The five rules

### 1. No witness

The top of any ladder must never require an audience. It measures depth, not who noticed.

The original instrument failed this on 37 out of 37 questions, without exception. Every top option was some version of: "people come to me", "others borrow my read", "I'm known for", "they've said so", "colleagues call me", "people preview decisions with me".

Why it matters: consider the reader who was made redundant last month. Or who started a new job in March. Or who moved from a team where she was the go-to person into one where nobody knows her yet. Her capability is unchanged. Her audience is gone. The instrument told her that her entire professional OS had degraded, across all five pillars at once.

That is the exact person the book is for. The tool contradicted the thesis at the moment the reader most needed it to hold.

Same applies to the capable individual contributor, the remote worker, the person in a culture where nobody asks for help, and the introvert who does excellent work quietly. All of them hit a permanent ceiling.

The line for interpersonal content: effect in the moment counts, reputation over time does not. "I can make someone feel understood mid-conflict" is a capability you carry into any room on day one. "People preview decisions with me before the meeting" is standing, and standing takes years and a stable seat.

Related failure modes, all the same bug in disguise:

- Seniority. "The room where it's decided", "resources have moved because of it", "your team". A capable IC is never in that room and never will be.
- Autonomy. "My conditions are engineered", "protect your mornings", "protect one recovery block". A carer, a shift worker, or someone in a support queue cannot engineer their conditions however skilled they are.
- Opportunity. "I've held the line when it cost me something", "it has held real weight when tested". This measures whether life has tested you. Someone values-led who has had a quieter decade is not less skilled.

### 2. Consistent and discerning, not louder

C can do the thing. D does it reliably and knows when not to.

No bravado. A first draft of the question rewrite overcorrected into "I pick fights with my own incompetence" and "I've made peace with occasionally being told off" - which swapped one proxy (audience) for another (personality). One of them actively rewarded boundary-crossing, which is poor judgment with good PR.

No absolutes: "reflex", "even mid-crisis", "the only real test", "operating at the highest level", "that's rare", "the marker of a genuinely senior professional". These encourage overclaiming and make the top feel heroic. The instrument is one self-report question per component. It cannot carry a verdict.

The discernment clause is usually what makes a top option work. Examples that landed:

- "I can tell when saying it out loud helps, and when it just makes things about me"
- "when one still holds up, I let it stay" (kills the reward for reflexive contrarianism)
- "I can tell the difference between permission I don't need and permission I do"
- "I know when twenty minutes will help and when what I really need is proper rest"

### 3. Lands cold, for a non-native English reader

The tool is reached by QR code and social links, with no book alongside it. A significant part of the readership thinks in English as a second language.

No insider references. No idioms doing load-bearing work. No regional slang ("I kinda dig it", "boy does that feel good", "easy mode", "not my lane"). No compressed office jargon ("under deadline" - "under pressure" is the construction a non-native expects, and "deadline" derails it). No unexplained abbreviations ("High EI").

This is not about lowering vocabulary. It is about not making a non-native reader work harder than a native one for the same question.

Things that survived, on Ioana's ear: "another day at the office", "isn't really on the menu", "move the needle", "blur word", "a draft, not a tattoo". When in doubt, ask her rather than sanding off a good line.

Where a term is worth keeping, let the sentence explain it rather than adding a gloss. Example: "I adapt eventually - usually once sunk cost has done its work: too far in to walk away now."

### 4. No back-references. Say the noun.

"The pattern" (which pattern?). "Look at one directly" (one what?). "Name it precisely" (name what?). "The scripts running". "The sustainability gap". "Constraints read as triggers" (triggers of what?).

A native reader auto-fills from context. A non-native reader stops and re-reads. Re-reading during a self-assessment is exactly when honest recognition leaks out and answer-management creeps in. The reader stops recognising themselves and starts working out what's being asked of them.

This rule came from Ioana.

### 5. No A option may read as a virtue

Questions recolour their neighbours. Check every low option against the items around it, not on its own.

Two real examples:

- After four Mindset questions rewarding flexibility, 1.9's "things change, I change with it" arrived sounding like agility. It's supposed to be the bottom of the Values ladder.
- In a pillar about examining beliefs, "those aren't beliefs, that's the wisdom to know how things stand" reads as experience.
- 2.7's old A said "Checking everything isn't realistic anyway" - which is true, and is very nearly D's own skill (proportionality). A offered a reasonable-sounding defence and quietly became pickable.

Also: the low option must not mock the reader. Gone: "Third time's the charm, surely", "the problem must be the problem", "If it's a chart, it's probably true". If A makes the reader the punchline, they answer aspirationally and the whole instrument drifts.

A low option should state the actual reasoning of the person choosing it, respectfully. They should be able to pick A without feeling stupid. Give them a real defence: "and they work...", "I'm not convinced I'm missing much."

## The b4 pattern

Every other assessment tops out at "now go teach it." Ours doesn't, and can't - see rule 1.

The pattern that works: the thing that makes you good at this is also the thing that lets it rot.

- Mindset: the beliefs you stopped examining are the ones going out of date
- Human Edge: a skill that only works in friendly conditions isn't finished
- Value Creation: the better you get, the easier it is to do valuable work that isn't yours
- Tech Fluency: fluency settles into a habit you've stopped examining
- Sustainability: it's the pillar people stop maintaining once it works

This is the growth mindset applied to itself. It is also the book's argument: experience is an asset, and unexamined experience quietly expires. And it's the only honest way to give a high scorer something to do alone.

Other legitimate directions for a top band: depth, unfamiliar contexts, the costly version, the parts running on autopilot.

A pattern is not a proof. The b4 pattern is a direction, not a template. If it shows up across many items, ask whether each one earns it independently, or whether some were written to fit. This already happened once: five pillar-level b4s all landed on "you stopped examining it", two of which hadn't earned that mechanism - and it was read as an intentional motif rather than a formula, because the structure was consistent. Consistency and correctness aren't the same signal. Applies to any pattern in this project, not just b4.

## Voice

Ioana's register: warmth with asides and parentheticals, occasional CAPS for emphasis, light emoji, spaced hyphens, never em dashes, no forced energy, no "hi guys it's meeee".

The humour lives in the questions and in the A and B options - not in D. Every joke in a top option is a small nudge toward picking it. A joke at the failure's expense is fine ("saying the same thing again, slower (or louder!)"); a flourish on D's virtue is not.

Emoji rule: questions may carry emoji, options may not. Options compete with each other, so an emoji on one is a thumb on the scale. Questions compete with nothing. (Currently no emoji in the set; Ioana may add them to question text later.)

The voice is part of the assessment mechanism, not decoration. It lowers the reader's defences and makes honest recognition more likely. Favour vivid truth over clinical precision, provided each step of the ladder stays defensible.

## Structural checks

- Tense consistency. If a question anchors to a moment ("this week", "the last group you worked with"), every option must sit in that tense. A split tense makes the general-claim option easier to pick than the specific-report ones. This bug appeared twice, in both directions.
- Construct drift. The top option must answer the question that was asked. Two real cases: 3.1 asked about business fluency and the top answered with power ("I make the case in the room where it's decided"). 3.5 asked where your energy went and the top answered about the org's priorities. The question quietly changed subject at the top.
- Pillar reports must describe the whole pillar. Human Edge's reports were entirely about emotional intelligence (1 component of 7). Tech Fluency's were entirely about AI (1 of 7). Someone strong at data thinking and weak at AI got a verdict about a subject they weren't tested on.
- Overlap. Check questions against their neighbours. 3.4 and 3.8 both asked "the thing didn't work, now what". 5.1 and 3.5 were near-identical ("where did your best energy go this week").
- Profiles describe the level. The shape note describes the balance. Profiles are assigned on the overall average alone, so they must not claim anything about balance - a flat 2.4 everywhere isn't uneven, and 4/4/4/4/2 averages 3.6 but isn't balanced. `spread.gapThreshold` and the shape note already handle this correctly.

## Working agreement

- Show diffs. Wait for approval. Never auto-push.
- Surgery over rewriting where existing text is good. Cut the clause that breaks a rule; don't rebuild the passage around it.
- One pillar at a time, then stop, unless told otherwise. Leave a REVIEW-NOTES file listing what changed and why.
- Test, don't reason. The two worst bugs in this project were invisible to code review and obvious the moment someone ran the thing: the Supabase write had never worked (401), and Kit's endpoint returns HTTP 200 even on failure. Reviews find code smells. Live runs find broken things.
- Flag, don't guess. If a rule and a good line seem to conflict, say so and let Ioana call it.
